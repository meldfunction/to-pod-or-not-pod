## THE PATTERN REPEATS

**JORDAN:** Last episode we talked about Jevons Paradox at a high level. Now let's get specific about the democratization pattern. Because it's not random. It repeats with mathematical precision.

**ALEX:** The 100x thing you mentioned?

**JORDAN:** Yes. Every major computing wave has been roughly 100x larger in unit volume and 10x cheaper per unit than the previous wave. And each wave discovered entirely new categories of work that couldn't exist before.

**MAYA:** Walk me through it chronologically. Because I lived through some of this but didn't see the pattern.

**JORDAN:** Let's start in 1970. The mainframe era.

---

## WAVE ONE: MAINFRAMES (1960s-1970s)

**JORDAN:** 1970. If you wanted computing power, you needed a mainframe.

**The Economics:**
- Price: $1 million+ per system
- Maintenance: $100K+/year
- Required specialized room with climate control
- Required dedicated operations team
- Total cost of ownership: multi-million dollar investment

**Who Could Afford:**
- Fortune 500 companies
- Government agencies
- Major universities  
- About 7,300 mainframes sold in 1979
- Total installed base worldwide: ~88,000

**What You Could Do:**
- Batch processing (submit jobs, wait for output)
- Accounting, payroll, inventory management
- Scientific calculations  
- Census data processing

**Who Did The Work:**
- Specialized operators (different job than programmers)
- Programmers who wrote in COBOL, FORTRAN
- Data entry clerks who prepared punch cards
- Computer room staff

**KARPATHY:** So the "computer" was a shared resource. Like a utility.

**JORDAN:** Exactly. You didn't have YOUR computer. You had access to THE computer. During your allocated time slot.

**MAYA:** And only giant organizations could afford this?

**JORDAN:** Yes. Which meant only giant organizations could automate. The local insurance agency? Still doing accounting by hand. The regional manufacturer? Still tracking inventory on paper. The small university? No computing access at all.

**Latent demand existed. But it was economically suppressed.**

---

## WAVE TWO: MINICOMPUTERS (1970s-1980s)

**JORDAN:** Then in the late 60s, Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC) introduced the minicomputer. Specifically the PDP series.

**The Economics:**
- Price: $18,000 - $100,000 (the PDP-8 was $18,000)
- Maintenance: Still significant but manageable
- Desk-sized instead of room-sized
- Could operate in normal office environment
- Total cost: accessible to departments, not just corporations

**The Numbers:**
- 81,300 minicomputers sold in 1979
- 100x more units than mainframes sold that year
- Different customer base entirely

**Who Could Now Afford:**
- Departments within large companies (engineering, R&D, finance)
- Mid-size companies (regional manufacturers, hospital systems)
- Small universities and research labs
- OEM manufacturers (embedding computers into larger systems)

**What NEW Work Became Possible:**
- Real-time process control (manufacturing automation)
- Scientific instrumentation and data collection
- Departmental computing (not waiting for mainframe time)
- Dedicated systems for specific functions
- Interactive computing (direct keyboard access)

**ALEX:** So this wasn't replacing mainframes?

**JORDAN:** Not at all. Mainframe sales kept growing. But minicomputers opened entirely NEW use cases. Process control in factories. Lab instrumentation. Departmental databases.

**Work that couldn't exist at mainframe prices.**

**MAYA:** And this created new jobs?

**JORDAN:** Completely new categories. "Process control engineer" wasn't a mainframe role. "Lab technician operating computerized instruments" didn't exist in mainframe era.

The work expanded to fill the new capacity.

---

## WAVE THREE: PERSONAL COMPUTERS (1980s-1990s)

**JORDAN:** Then came the PC revolution. Apple II in 1977, IBM PC in 1981.

**The Economics:**
- Price: $10,000 → $5,000 → $2,000 over the decade
- No maintenance staff needed
- Fit on a desk
- Individual could operate
- Total cost: accessible to knowledge workers

**The Numbers:**
- 329,000 microcomputers sold in 1979
- Millions sold annually by mid-1980s
- By 1984, PC sales ($11.6B) exceeded mainframe sales ($11.4B) for first time in history
- 100x more units than minicomputers (eventually)

**Who Could Now Afford:**
- Individual knowledge workers
- Small businesses (mom-and-pop shops, solo professionals)
- Students
- Eventually, households

**What NEW Work Became Possible:**
- Personal productivity (word processing, spreadsheets)
- Desktop publishing
- Small business accounting
- Computer-aided design (CAD)
- Personal financial management
- Educational software
- Video games creating entire industry

**KARPATHY:** And this is when "computer literacy" became a thing?

**JORDAN:** Yes. Because the computer was now YOUR tool, not A tool you accessed through intermediaries.

**The Job Transformation:**

**Disappeared:**
- Typing pools (word processing made secretaries more productive)
- Manual bookkeepers (spreadsheets automated calculations)
- Drafters (CAD replaced manual technical drawing)

**Created:**
- Desktop publishers
- Spreadsheet analysts
- Personal computer trainers
- Software support specialists
- Computer sales and service

**MAYA:** Net effect on jobs?

**JORDAN:** Massively positive. The work that COULD be done expanded so dramatically that even though individual tasks got automated, total employment in knowledge work grew.

**SAGE:** Classic Jevons. Efficiency revealed latent demand.

---

## WAVE FOUR: CLOUD / SaaS (2000s-2010s)

**LEVIE:** This is my era. And the pattern continued.

**The Economics:**
- Price: $50-500/month subscription (vs $10K upfront for software)
- Zero maintenance (provider handles it)
- No installation (access via web browser)
- No IT staff needed
- Total cost: accessible to ANY business

**The Numbers:**
- Billions of users globally
- Every business, regardless of size
- 100x more accessible than desktop software

**Who Could Now Afford:**
- Every barbershop, plumber, consultant, freelancer
- Non-profits with tiny budgets
- International users in developing economies
- Anyone with internet access

**What NEW Work Became Possible:**
- Sophisticated CRM for small businesses
- Marketing automation for solopreneurs
- Project management for distributed teams
- Video conferencing replacing travel
- Online education at scale
- Gig economy platforms (Uber, Airbnb, Upwork)

**LEVIE:** "While you would have to have been a Fortune 500 company to access powerful software to do your accounting in the 1970s, by the 2000s with the cloud, it was available to every barbershop in the world."

**ALEX:** And this created the "solopreneur" category?

**LEVIE:** Exactly. One person with cloud tools could operate like a small company used to. That wasn't possible before.

**MAYA:** But did this eliminate jobs?

**JORDAN:** Let me give you data. Software industry employment:

- 1990: ~1.5 million in US
- 2000: ~2.5 million  
- 2010: ~3.5 million
- 2020: ~4.5 million

**Making software cheaper and more accessible tripled employment in software. Because it exploded the NUMBER of businesses using software.**

---

**KARPATHY:** So you're saying AI is the next 100x wave?

**JORDAN:** The pattern suggests it.

**The Economics (Emerging):**
- Price: Pennies per task (vs dollars per hour for human labor)
- No hiring, training, onboarding
- Instant deployment
- Scales infinitely
- Total cost: accessible to individuals, not just businesses

**Who Can Now Afford:**
- Literally anyone with API access

**The work reveals itself AFTER the capability becomes accessible.**

**LEVIE:** "The vast majority of AI tokens in the future will be used on things we don't even do today as workers."

**MAYA:** But you must have some guesses?

**Wave 1 (Mainframes):** Only Fortune 500 could automate accounting

**ALEX:** So the 10-person company can have:

## THE EMPLOYMENT QUESTION

**KARPATHY:** But I still don't see how this creates jobs instead of eliminating them.

**JORDAN:** Let me show you the parallel.

**1970s worry:** "Minicomputers will eliminate mainframe operators!"  
**What happened:** Mainframe operators stayed employed. But thousands of NEW jobs appeared for minicomputer operators, process control engineers, lab technicians.

**1980s worry:** "PCs will eliminate secretaries!"  
**What happened:** Secretarial pools shrank. But millions of NEW jobs appeared for knowledge workers using PCs directly.

**2000s worry:** "Cloud software will eliminate IT departments!"  
**What happened:** Enterprise IT staff shifted roles. But millions of NEW jobs appeared for cloud architects, SaaS companies, remote workers.

**2020s worry:** "AI will eliminate knowledge workers!"  
**What will happen (if pattern holds):** Some tasks get automated. But ??? NEW jobs appear for ???

**MAYA:** We can't predict the new jobs because they require the new capability to exist first.

**JORDAN:** Exactly. "Social media manager" wasn't a job category until social media existed. "Cloud security specialist" wasn't a job until cloud computing existed. "Prompt engineer" wasn't a job until LLMs existed.

**The capability creates the category.**

---

## THE TIMING QUESTION

**ALEX:** How long does each wave take?

**JORDAN:** Here's the interesting pattern:

**Mainframes (1950s-1970s):** ~20 year era of dominance  
**Minicomputers (1970s-1990s):** ~20 year era  
**PCs (1980s-2010s):** ~30 year era  
**Cloud (2000s-2020s):** ~20 year era  
**AI (2020s-???)**

**Each wave doesn't REPLACE the previous wave. It ADDS to it.**

**SAGE:** Mainframes still exist. Minicomputer architecture still used in embedded systems. PCs still everywhere. Cloud still growing.

**MAYA:** So AI doesn't eliminate cloud computing?

**JORDAN:** No. It adds a layer. AI runs ON cloud infrastructure. Just like cloud ran ON PCs and networks. Just like PCs ran alongside minicomputers and mainframes.

**It's not replacement. It's expansion.**

---

## THE UNCOMFORTABLE TRUTH

**LEVIE:** "Of course, many are wondering what happens to all the jobs in this new world? The reality is that despite all the tasks that AI lets us automate, it still requires people to pull together the full workflow to produce real value."

**KARPATHY:** What does that mean practically?

**LEVIE:** "AI agents require management, oversight, and substantial context to get the full gains."

**JORDAN:** Think about what happened with Excel. Excel automated calculations that bookkeepers used to do by hand. Did this eliminate bookkeepers?

**ALEX:** Some, yes. But it also enabled financial analysts who could do sophisticated modeling that wasn't possible before.

**JORDAN:** Right. The TASK of "adding numbers" got automated. But the WORK of "understanding financial implications" expanded.

**Today's jobs become tomorrow's tasks.**

**MAYA:** So "junior programmer who implements features" becomes a task that senior engineers do with AI assistance?

**KARPATHY:** And what's the new job that emerges?

**JORDAN:** We don't know yet. But history suggests: orchestrator roles. System designer roles. Context provider roles. Quality assurance roles. Roles that require human judgment about WHAT to build, even if AI handles HOW to build it.

**When X becomes abundant, Y becomes valuable:**

**Wave 1:** When computing power becomes abundant (via mainframes) → programming skill becomes valuable

**Wave 2:** When departmental computing becomes abundant (via minis) → system design becomes valuable

**Wave 3:** When personal computing becomes abundant (via PCs) → computer literacy becomes valuable

**Wave 4:** When software becomes abundant (via cloud) → digital strategy becomes valuable

**JORDAN:** What becomes scarce when intelligence is abundant?

**ALEX:** Wisdom?

**LEVIE:** All of the above. Plus: the ability to figure out WHAT problems are worth solving. The ability to convince people to change behavior. The ability to navigate human systems that resist change.

## THE 10 VS 100 PERSON COMPANY

**ALEX:** Let me test this with my company. Right now, 10 people. We can't afford:

- Full legal team
- Marketing department
- Software developers
- Customer success team
- Market research

**With AI, I could potentially access all those capabilities. What happens?**

**JORDAN:** Two scenarios:

**Scenario A - Replacement:**
You do the same work with fewer people. 10-person company becomes 5-person company doing the same volume.

**Scenario B - Expansion:**
You do 10x more work with the same 10 people. Serve more customers, enter new markets, build new products.

**Which happens?**

**ALEX:** Based on Jevons Paradox... expansion?

**JORDAN:** If the pattern holds, yes. Because the CONSTRAINT wasn't "can we do this work?" It was "can we afford to try?"

Now you can afford to try. So you try 10x more things. Some succeed. Your company grows. You hire more people to manage the expanded scope.

**MAYA:** But other companies are doing the same thing. More competition?

**JORDAN:** Yes. But also more TOTAL value being created. The pie is growing, not just being divided differently.

**LEVIE:** "Even as AI progresses to accomplish more of an entire workflow, we will simply expect more from the work that we're doing."

**The goalposts move. The work expands to fill the capacity.**
