## THE FUNDAMENTAL QUESTION

**What kind of value CAN'T be extracted?**

**MAYA:** What do you mean "can't be extracted"?

**SAGE:** Most business models are extractive. You create value, capture it, sell it, scale it. The value exists independent of the relationship between creator and consumer.

**But some value only exists THROUGH relationship. It can't be extracted or scaled. It requires participation.**

**ALEX:** Give me an example.

**ELDER:** The barn raising.

## THE BARN RAISING PRINCIPLE

**ELDER:** In our communities, when someone needs a barn, the community builds it together. One day, forty people, barn complete.

**ALEX:** That's efficient.

**ELDER:** The efficiency isn't the point. The RELATIONSHIP is the point.

**MAYA:** I don't understand.

**ELDER:** The barn is the byproduct. The community cohesion is the product.

**You could hire contractors to build the barn more professionally. But you'd lose:**
- The strengthening of relationships through working together
- The reciprocal obligation created (next time, we help you)
- The knowledge transfer (younger members learn from elders)
- The belonging felt through collective achievement
- The trust built through shared effort

**None of that exists if you just hire contractors.**

**SAGE:** The value only exists through participation. You can't extract "barn raising cohesion" and sell it. It only exists in the doing.

**JORDAN:** And this is fundamentally different from extractive value models.

---

## EXTRACTIVE VS PARTICIPATORY VALUE

**JORDAN:** Let me diagram the difference.

**EXTRACTIVE VALUE MODEL:**

1. Create product/service (independent of customer)
2. Sell to customer (transaction)
3. Capture profit (extraction)
4. Scale by replication (sell to more customers)
5. Value exists independent of relationship

**Example:** SaaS product
- Build once
- Sell to thousands
- Value is the software, not the relationship
- Customer gets same value whether they know you or not
- Scalable through replication

**PARTICIPATORY VALUE MODEL:**

1. Create value THROUGH relationship (requires both parties)
2. Participate together (ongoing engagement)
3. Share outcomes (mutual benefit)
4. Scale through networks (not replication)
5. Value only exists in the relationship

**Example:** Barn raising
- Build together
- Can't sell to thousands
- Value is the relationship, barn is byproduct
- Participants get different value than observers
- Can't scale through replication (requires actual participation)

**MAYA:** So extractive = scalable but vulnerable to automation. Participatory = unscalable but AI-resistant?

**SAGE:** Exactly.

---

## WHY THIS MATTERS FOR AI DISRUPTION

**ALEX:** Okay, but I'm not Amish. I run a business. How does this apply?

**SAGE:** AI can replicate extractive value. It can't replicate participatory value.

**Examples:**

**Extractive (AI can replicate):**
- Design a logo (product exists independent of relationship)
- Write marketing copy (deliverable is the value)
- Generate code (output is separable from creator)
- Analyze data (insights are extractable)

**Participatory (AI cannot replicate):**
- Understand our company's design needs through five years of collaboration (value is the accumulated context)
- Navigate our team's communication style to get buy-in (value is relationship knowledge)
- Architect solutions that fit our specific culture (value is deep knowing)
- Hold space for difficult strategic conversations (value is presence)

**JORDAN:** See the pattern? When value can be extracted from the relationship, AI can do it. When value only exists IN the relationship, AI can assist but not replace.

**MAYA:** So the move is: shift from extractive to participatory business models?

**SAGE:** If you want to be AI-resistant, yes.

**MORGAN:** I've been quiet because this is exactly what I discovered during my six months of "doing nothing."

**MORGAN:** I was a designer. Extractive model. Client hires me, I design thing, they pay, we're done. The value was the deliverable.

But during my break, I kept having conversations with other designers about a shared problem: clients who can't articulate what they want but reject everything you show them.

**MAYA:** That's every client.

**MORGAN:** Right. And I realized: the VALUE isn't the design. The value is helping clients discover what they actually want. That's participatory. It only happens through relationship.

**SAGE:** This is the care work insight.

**MORGAN:** What do you mean?

**SAGE:** Care work has always been participatory. You can't extract "caring for someone" and sell it separately. The value IS the participation.

**Think about:**
- Childcare (value is the relationship with specific caregivers)
- Elder care (value is dignity and presence, not tasks)
- Teaching (value is developmental relationship, not information transfer)
- Therapy (value is being witnessed, not techniques)

**All of these are 92-98% AI-resistant because the value requires participation.**

**JORDAN:** And what Morgan discovered is: most knowledge work has a participatory core that's been hidden by extractive framing.

## HIDDEN PARTICIPATORY VALUE

**JORDAN:** Most businesses think they're selling deliverables. But the actual value is often participatory.

**Examples:**

**What you think you sell:** Website design  
**What clients actually value:** Someone who understands their business and translates that into visual form through ongoing dialogue

**What you think you sell:** Marketing strategy  
**What clients actually value:** A thinking partner who challenges their assumptions and helps them see what they're missing

**What you think you sell:** Code implementation  
**What clients actually value:** An engineer who learns their codebase deeply and makes thoughtful architectural decisions

**What you think you sell:** Consulting advice  
**What clients actually value:** Trusted relationship where they can think out loud without judgment

**MAYA:** So the deliverable is just... evidence of the relationship?

**SAGE:** Often, yes. The report, the design, the code—those are artifacts. The value is the PROCESS of creating them together.

**ELDER:** This is what we've always known. You don't hire someone to build a fence. You build it together. The fence proves you can work together. The relationship is why you hired them.

## THE THREE PROPERTIES OF PARTICIPATORY VALUE

**SAGE:** Participatory value has three properties that make it AI-resistant:

**Property 1: Value exists only in the relationship**

You can't take it and sell it elsewhere. The barn raising cohesion only exists for the people who participated. The client relationship only exists through years of working together.

**Property 2: Cannot be extracted or replicated**

You can't copy participatory value like you copy software. Each relationship is unique to its participants. The knowledge Morgan built about her client's unstated preferences can't be transferred to another designer.

**Property 3: Requires ongoing participation to maintain**

Stop participating → lose the value. If you stop attending barn raisings, you lose access to mutual aid. If you stop engaging with clients, you lose the relationship.

**JORDAN:** And these three properties are exactly what makes it durable in the AI age.

**AI can generate deliverables. But it can't participate. It has no skin in the game.**

## SKIN IN THE GAME

**ALEX:** What does "skin in the game" mean here?

**ELDER:** Real friendship requires mutual vulnerability. Both parties risk something. You change each other.

Can AI be vulnerable? Can it risk something? Can it be changed by you?

**ALEX:** No.

**ELDER:** Then it can't participate. It can simulate. But simulation isn't participation.

**MAYA:** So even if AI gets so good it passes every Turing test, it still can't do care work?

**SAGE:** Right. Because care work isn't about convincing someone you care. It's about ACTUALLY caring. Which requires having something at stake.

**The caregiver caring for your dying parent has emotional stakes. They're affected by your parent's suffering.**

**The AI caregiver has no stakes. It simulates empathy but doesn't experience it.**

**And humans can tell the difference. Not through testing. Through felt sense.**

**JORDAN:** This is why parasocial relationships with AI will always be asymmetric. You can be affected by the AI. The AI cannot be affected by you.

## BUSINESS MODEL IMPLICATIONS

**MAYA:** Okay, I'm convinced this is real. But how do I actually build a business on participatory value?

**SAGE:** Three models:

**Model 1: Worker Cooperatives**  
Shared ownership among workers. Value created through collective participation. Profits distributed among participants.

**Example:** Design cooperative where designers collectively own the firm, share clients, learn from each other, distribute profits equally.

**Why it works:** The value is the collective knowledge and relationships. Can't be extracted by outside capital. Requires ongoing participation.

**Model 2: Mutual Aid Networks**  
Reciprocal support systems. Share resources, skills, knowledge. No transaction, no debt—gift economy.

**Example:** Childcare co-op where five families rotate care, share costs, build relationships between families.

**Why it works:** The value is the trust and relationships built over time. Can't be replicated by service providers. Requires participation from all families.

**Model 3: Membership Communities**  
Ongoing engagement over time. Value accrues through participation. Can't join halfway and get same value.

**Example:** Professional learning community where members share experiments monthly, build collective knowledge, support each other through transitions.

**Why it works:** The value is accumulated context and relationships. New members can't buy their way into the history. Requires time and participation.

**ALEX:** What about pricing? How do you charge for participatory value?

**JORDAN:** Different economic logic entirely.

## ECONOMICS OF PARTICIPATION

**JORDAN:** Extractive and participatory value have different economics.

**EXTRACTIVE VALUE ECONOMICS:**

- Price based on deliverable
- High volume × low margin = revenue
- Scale through replication
- Optimize for efficiency
- Commodification risk high

**Example:** $5K website × 50 clients/year = $250K revenue
- Can serve 50 clients because you're just delivering product
- But vulnerable to AI doing it cheaper

**PARTICIPATORY VALUE ECONOMICS:**

- Price based on relationship depth/length
- Low volume × high margin = revenue
- Scale through network effects
- Optimize for trust and context
- Commodification risk low

**Example:** $25K/year ongoing relationship × 10 clients = $250K revenue
- Can only serve 10 clients because requires deep engagement
- But AI can't replicate the accumulated context

**MAYA:** So participatory work pays more per client but serves fewer clients?

**JORDAN:** Usually, yes. Because you're charging for:
- Years of accumulated context
- Deep understanding of client's unstated needs
- Trust built through previous projects
- Being available for ongoing navigation

**You can't serve fifty clients at that depth. But you don't need to.**

## THE TRANSITION STRATEGY

**ALEX:** How do I transition from extractive to participatory business model?

**SAGE:** Four steps:

**Step 1: Identify your current participatory value**

What do clients actually value beyond deliverables? What relationship-based value are you already creating but not charging for?

**For designers:** Understanding their brand deeply over time  
**For consultants:** Being trusted thinking partners  
**For engineers:** Making architectural decisions that fit their culture

**Step 2: Make it explicit**

Stop hiding participatory value inside extractive transactions. Name it. "What I'm really offering is ongoing strategic partnership, not one-off deliverables."

**Step 3: Price for depth, not volume**

Instead of: $5K per project × 50 projects  
Try: $25K/year ongoing relationship × 10 clients

Charge for the relationship, the accumulated context, the availability, the trust.

**Step 4: Build cooperative infrastructure**

You can't do this alone. Form cooperative with 3-5 others doing similar transitions. Share learnings. Support each other through the scary middle.

**MORGAN:** This is what I'm experimenting with now. Instead of "hire me to design your website," I'm trying "let's work together for a year to figure out what your brand actually is."

**MAYA:** How's it going?

**MORGAN:** Scary. But I'm serving three clients at depth instead of thirty clients superficially. And the work feels meaningful again.

**Because I'm participating in their becoming, not just delivering artifacts.**

## THE AMISH MODEL APPLIED

**ELDER:** This is exactly our economic model. We don't extract value. We participate in value creation.

**Examples from our communities:**

**Quilting circles:** Women make quilts together. Could you hire someone to make a quilt cheaper? Yes. But you'd lose the social fabric (literally and metaphorically) created through collective making.

**Barn raisings:** Could you hire contractors? Yes. But you'd lose the mutual obligation and community cohesion.

**Shared equipment:** We collectively own expensive equipment (grain threshers, sawmills). Could each family buy their own? Some could. But collective ownership requires participation in maintenance and decision-making. That participation creates relationship.

**Mutual aid:** When harvest fails or disaster strikes, the community provides. Not charity—reciprocal obligation. You participate in helping others, they participate in helping you.

**ALEX:** So your whole economy is participatory?

**ELDER:** Mostly. We have some extractive transactions (selling goods to outsiders). But WITHIN the community, value flows through participation and relationship.

**And that's why we're resilient. You can't extract Amish community value and sell it. It only exists through participation.**

**AI can't automate our economy because the value IS the participation.**

## THE HARD TRUTH

**MAYA:** This all sounds great. But here's the hard question:

**Participatory business models seem less profitable. I could serve more clients with extractive model. Why should I make less money?**

**SAGE:** Three responses.

**Response 1: You might not make less money**

Depth charges higher rates. $25K/year × 10 clients = $250K. Same revenue as $5K × 50 clients. But less marketing, less client management, less context switching.

You might make SAME money with better quality of life.

**Response 2: Profit isn't the only metric**

What do you actually want? If it's maximum profit, extractive might win (until AI commodifies you). But if it's meaningful work, sustainable pace, genuine relationships—participatory wins.

**Response 3: This isn't moral. It's strategic.**

In stable markets, extractive models win on efficiency. In phase transitions, participatory models win on resilience.

**When the ground is shifting, you don't want the tallest building. You want the deepest roots.**

**JORDAN:** The question isn't "should I be noble and make less money?" It's "what survives AI disruption?"

And the answer is: value that requires participation.

Pilot the participatory model. Document what works/fails. Adjust.

**Month 6: Evaluate**

Is this more sustainable? More meaningful? More resilient? If yes, transition more clients. If no, refine.
